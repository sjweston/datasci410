\subsection{\texorpdfstring{The data:
\texttt{mpg}}{The data: mpg}}\label{the-data-mpg}

This dataset has fuel economy data for 234 cars. Here are 8 rows with
the columns you'll need:

jeep & grand cherokee 4wd & 17 & 22 & d \\
chevrolet & k1500 tahoe 4wd & 11 & 14 & e \\
chevrolet & c1500 suburban 2wd & 14 & 20 & r \\
\end{longtable}

\textbf{Key:} \texttt{cty} = city miles per gallon, \texttt{hwy} =
highway miles per gallon, \texttt{fl} = fuel type (d = diesel, e =
ethanol, p = premium, r = regular)

\begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{0.5pt}\end{center}

\subsection{The task (same as the slide
exercise)}\label{the-task-same-as-the-slide-exercise}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Plot \texttt{cty} (x-axis) vs \texttt{hwy} (y-axis)
\item
  Color points by \textbf{fuel type} (\texttt{fl})
\item
  Add a smooth trend line
\item
  Give it a title and axis labels
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Check your work}\label{check-your-work}

Compare your hand-drawn plot and your code with your partner's screen.
Did you get the general pattern right?

\textbf{Expected code:}

\begin{verbatim}
ggplot(mpg, aes(x = cty, y = hwy, color = fl)) +
  geom_point() +
  geom_smooth() +
  labs(
    title = "City vs Highway MPG by Fuel Type",
    x = "City MPG",
    y = "Highway MPG",
    color = "Fuel type"
  )
\end{verbatim}

\textbf{Expected pattern:} Yes --- city and highway MPG are strongly
positively related. Cars that get good mileage in the city also get good
mileage on the highway.
